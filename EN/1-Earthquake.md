# The Earthquake
**By:** Ali Hashemian  
**Suggested by:** Dr. Masoud Seddighin  

*I highly recommend reading this text to the end; it will be one of the most unconventional pieces you have ever read!*

Imagine one night, everything suddenly comes to an end. Midnight, Tehran, an 8.0 magnitude earthquake. My friend and I are in the house. The ceiling of the room holds, but a wall collapses on his leg. His leg is broken. Power and internet are out, screams are heard from every direction, but the absolute darkness prevents us from seeing the scale of the disaster. I don't know what kind of game I am trapped in; a passing crisis of a few hours or an endless nightmare of several weeks? My first constraint is clear right now: my friend cannot move. This means I cannot flee either. I am chained to this semi-ruined house.

We only have food and water for two days. I must decide whether to go out right now to scavenge for supplies or wait until morning? Going out in this darkness and chaos is dangerous, but if I wait, everything might be looted by morning. This is the first dilemma. The first mover acquires the most resources, but might also be the first victim of violence. Out of fear, I choose to stay.

In the morning, in front of the damaged shops, I witnessed a collective hesitation. Despite the collapse of the law, people still refrained from stealing due to the inertia of their past behavior. This shows that in the initial hours of a crisis, the norms of the old world still govern minds. It was as if everyone was waiting to see who would make the first move. Their minds were still operating in a world where stealing equaled prison and order was a permanent habit. These norms are, in fact, old **Nash Equilibria** that were once stable due to the presence of police and law. Although the foundation of this order is gone, its inertia remains.

The first few people shopped cautiously; for a few minutes, a spontaneous order had formed based on the thought that "if I follow the rules, others will too." But suddenly, the eighth or ninth person grabs a whole box of water and runs. Just one person is enough. With his move, the dam breaks and everyone rushes inside. Within minutes, the shop is emptied. This is a **Cascading Effect**; where collective behavior is determined not by individual logic, but by the first moves of others. Order was a bubble that burst with a single needle. This perfectly illustrates a game with **Multiple Equilibria**. Both "order" and "looting" were equilibria. Which equilibrium prevails depends on the behavior of the first actors. In an atmosphere of fear and insecurity, the negative equilibrium is more likely, because fear makes everyone assume that others will loot. Furthermore, the social network structure determines which equilibrium forms. In traditional neighborhoods where people have known each other for years, cooperation forms more easily because the game has already been repeated. But in modern towers where neighbors don't know each other, every encounter is a **One-Shot Game**, and the probability of defection and looting is much higher.

Days two and three passed with no sign of rescue. Now I understand the rules of the game: survival, without a referee, and without a defined end. I have physical strength, but because I have to look after my injured friend, I cannot risk physical confrontation. This is where one must think of the **Hawk-Dove Game**. In the competition over resources, anyone can be a "Hawk" (aggressive) or a "Dove" (accommodating). If one is a Hawk and the other a Dove, the Hawk takes everything. But if both are Hawks, a clash occurs and both suffer severe damage. I cannot afford the risk of being a Hawk, but being merely a Dove means starving to death. A third way must be found: diplomacy, bargaining, and intelligence instead of brute force.

The old neighbor has food but no water. I know the location of a water valve that still works. I make a deal with the old man: water for food. How does trust form? The answer lies in repetition. Because we both need each other every day, today's betrayal means the destruction of tomorrow's resource. The repeated game itself acts as a guarantor of honesty. This is the exact logic of the **Folk Theorem**: in repeated games, if the future is important enough, cooperation can become a stable equilibrium. Another point is that I do not reveal the location of the water valve so the old man remains dependent on me. I keep it hidden; not out of malice, but to maintain **Bargaining Power**. In these conditions, information is the only asset that can be protected without physical force.

On the fourth day, a group of families gathered to establish an order for division of labor and keeping watch. The group leader considered my injured friend a **Free-Rider** (a consumer with no output) and refused to accept us. I approach this from a different angle. My care for my injured friend is a signal in itself. I tell them that someone who doesn't abandon his friend in this situation will not abandon you either. This means **Costly Signaling**. Claiming loyalty is cheap, but someone who practically pays the heavy price of loyalty makes a much more credible claim. Because this action is costly, it is hard to fake, and it builds trust. Ultimately, they accept me, on the condition that I work twice as much as the others.

On the fifth day, resources are running out. I see a young man carrying a bag of bread. He is alone and weaker than me. If I take his bread, I am insured for a few days. The risk of confrontation is also almost zero. I pause. From the perspective of mathematical logic, stealing the bread is the **Dominant Strategy**. But I hold back. Why? Out of conscience. Was this conscience a calculation error? Perhaps. But perhaps conscience is an evolutionary shortcut to equilibrium that has been ingrained in our brains through long-term repeated games. Untrustworthy individuals are eventually eliminated. But the bitter irony is that in a short-term crisis, conscience might be a mistake from the viewpoint of Game Theory, but from the viewpoint of humanity, it is the only thing we have left. Sometimes, one must choose between a Nash Equilibrium and staying human. Mathematical models cannot tell you which is more important.

In the following days, more powerful groups formed. A group of youths took control of the neighborhood warehouse and demanded a share in exchange for security. Is this extortion, or the formation of a primitive state? Hobbes says exactly this in *Leviathan*. In the absence of a central power, people, out of fear of a war of all against all, surrender a part of their freedom to a stronger power in order to survive. Those youths have no legitimacy and were not elected, but they have executive power and brute force. Within a week, the structure of taxation-for-security was spontaneously reborn.

On the ninth day, a rumor spread that help was arriving soon. It doesn't matter if it was true or not; the mere belief was enough for a collapse. Until today, cooperation was built on the assumption that we would need each other tomorrow. But when the probability of the game continuing disappears, the **Discount Factor** of the future drops to zero. When players feel the game has reached its final round, the incentive to defect peaks. This is the **End-Game Effect**; just like the last round in the Prisoner's Dilemma, where there is no longer a reason for loyalty. That night, two people from our group fled with a large portion of the supplies because they thought we had reached the final round.

Ten days have passed since that midnight. We are tired, hungry, and exhausted, but alive. When I look back at these ten days, I see that the main disaster wasn't the earthquake. Society regressed from civilization to barbarism in a matter of days; not because people became bad, but because the structure of the game changed. People who respected each other in a bakery line before the earthquake are now fighting over a bottle of water. They haven't changed; their **Payoff Matrix** has changed. When the cost of defection is zero and its reward is survival, defection becomes the **Dominant Strategy**.

The greatest lesson of this experience for me was this: civilization is not made of concrete buildings and stone walls. Civilization is a fragile equilibrium; built from trust, institutions, and mechanisms that make cooperation profitable. An earthquake destroys buildings, but what is truly hard to rebuild is this shattered equilibrium.
